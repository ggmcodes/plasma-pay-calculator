California-Specific Plasma Donation Regulations

California plasma centers must comply with both FDA federal regulations and state-specific requirements that protect donor safety and rights.

California Donor Requirements

Age and identification:

  • Must be 18 years or older (no exceptions in California)
  • Valid government-issued photo ID required (driver's license, state ID, passport)
  • Proof of Social Security number (card or official document)
  • Proof of current California address (utility bill, bank statement, lease within 30 days)

Health requirements:

  • Minimum weight: 110 pounds
  • Pass physical examination and health screening
  • Protein levels must meet minimum standards (tested at each visit)
  • Blood pressure and pulse within acceptable ranges
  • No recent tattoos or piercings (varies by center, typically 3-12 month wait)

California Donation Limits

California follows FDA regulations for donation frequency:

  • Maximum frequency: Twice per week with at least 2 full days (48 hours) between donations
  • Monthly maximum: No more than 2 donations in a 7-day period
  • Annual volume limits: Based on weight and protein levels, tracked electronically

All California centers are connected to a national database that tracks your donations across all facilities, preventing over-donation even if you visit multiple centers.

California Labor and Payment Rules

Payment structure:

  • Plasma "donation" is technically compensation for time, not payment for plasma itself
  • Centers typically pay via prepaid debit card loaded immediately after donation
  • Some centers offer direct deposit or digital payment apps
  • You can donate at multiple centers but must track frequency yourself for safety

Tax considerations:

  • Plasma compensation is taxable income in California
  • Centers will issue 1099-MISC if you earn $600+ in a calendar year
  • Track your earnings for state and federal tax filing
  • Consult tax professional about deductions for mileage, supplies

California Consumer Protections

California offers some of the strongest consumer protections for plasma donors:

  • Privacy rights: HIPAA plus California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) protections
  • Medical screening: Required physical exam before first donation and annually
  • Informed consent: Centers must fully explain risks and procedures
  • Adverse event reporting: Centers must report complications to state health officials
  • Facility standards: Regular inspections by California Department of Public Health
