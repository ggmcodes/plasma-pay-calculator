Is Plasma Donation Income Taxable?

Yes, absolutely. Despite what you may have heard, the IRS clearly considers plasma donation compensation as taxable income. Here's why:

  • It's not a "donation" - You're being compensated for your time, not donating for free
  • It's classified as "other income" - Reported on Schedule 1 of your tax return
  • All amounts are taxable - Even if under $600 and no 1099 received
IRS Warning

Failing to report plasma income is tax evasion. Plasma centers report payments to the IRS, and you can be audited for unreported income.

1099 Rules for Plasma Donation

You'll receive a 1099-NEC or 1099-MISC if you earned $600 or more from a single plasma center in a calendar year.

What If You Don't Get a 1099?

If you earned less than $600 at a center, or if the center failed to send a 1099, you're still required to report the income. Keep your own records:

  • Save all payment receipts and statements
  • Track donations in a spreadsheet
  • Screenshot your center app earnings history

How to Report Plasma Income on Your Taxes

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Gather your 1099s from each plasma center
  2. Add up total earnings from all centers
  3. Report on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Line 8z as "Other Income"
  4. Write "Plasma Donation" as the description
  5. Calculate deductions (see below)
Tax Software Tip

In TurboTax or H&R Block, search for "other income" or "miscellaneous income" and enter your plasma earnings there.

Set aside approximately 15-25% of your plasma earnings for taxes, depending on your tax bracket. A simple formula:

Common Plasma Tax Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Not reporting income under $600 - It's still taxable even without a 1099
  2. Forgetting mileage deductions - This can save you hundreds
  3. Not keeping records - Track all donations and payments
  4. Ignoring estimated taxes - Can result in penalties
  5. Claiming it as a charitable donation - It's not; you were paid
