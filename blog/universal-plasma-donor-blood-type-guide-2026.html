Quick Answer

For plasma, type AB is the universal donor: AB plasma carries no anti-A or anti-B antibodies, so it can be given to patients of any blood type. This is the exact opposite of whole-blood donation, where O-negative is the universal donor. Your blood type does not change how much you're paid for plasma — pay is based on your visit and weight.

If you've heard that “O-negative is the universal donor,” you're right — but only for blood. Plasma flips the rule completely. Understanding which blood type is the universal plasma donor clears up one of the most common points of confusion among donors, and answers the question everyone really wants to know: does your blood type change your pay?

Who Is the Universal Plasma Donor?

For plasma, the universal donor is type AB (AB-positive or AB-negative). Here's why: your plasma is the liquid part of blood that carries antibodies. People with type AB blood don't make anti-A or anti-B antibodies, so their plasma won't react against a recipient's red cells. That means AB plasma can be transfused safely to a patient of any blood type — A, B, AB, or O.

Because only about 4% of people are AB, AB plasma is in particular demand for hospital transfusions and is sometimes specifically recruited.

Why It's the Opposite of Blood Donation

This trips a lot of people up, so here's the clean version:

  • Red blood cells: the universal donor is O-negative, because O-negative red cells have no A, B, or Rh antigens for a recipient's antibodies to attack.
  • Plasma: the universal donor is AB, because AB plasma has no anti-A or anti-B antibodies to attack a recipient's red cells.

In short: with red cells you care about antigens (O wins); with plasma you care about antibodies (AB wins). They're mirror images.

Does Your Blood Type Affect How Much You Get Paid?

For ordinary plasma donation at a commercial center: no. Your compensation is based on your donation visit, current promotions, and often your body weight — not your ABO/Rh type. An O-positive donor and an AB-negative donor of the same weight, at the same center, on the same promotion, earn the same. Estimate yours with the plasma pay calculator.

The nuance: some specialty antibody programs (below) recruit specific donors and may pay differently, but standard pay charts don't vary by blood type.

Special Plasma Programs by Antibody Type

A few programs do care about your blood or antibodies:

  • Anti-D / Rh-negative programs. Some centers run specialized programs for donors whose plasma contains certain antibodies (for example, used to make Rh immune globulin). These are separate, invitation-based programs — see our anti-D program guide.
  • AB plasma demand. Because AB plasma is universal and rare, it's especially valued for transfusion medicine.

For the typical donor, though, you don't need to know your blood type to donate or to get paid.

How to Find Out Your Blood Type

You usually don't need to know your blood type to donate plasma — the center doesn't require it for standard donation. If you're curious, you can find it on past blood-donation records, ask your doctor, check results from a previous lab test, or use an at-home blood-typing kit. Whatever your type, if you meet the eligibility requirements you can donate and earn.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which blood type is the universal plasma donor?

Type AB is the universal plasma donor. AB plasma contains no anti-A or anti-B antibodies, so it can be safely given to patients of any blood type. Since only about 4% of people are AB, AB plasma is in high demand.

Isn't O-negative the universal donor?

O-negative is the universal donor for red blood cells, not plasma. Plasma is the opposite: AB is universal for plasma because it lacks anti-A and anti-B antibodies. The rule flips because red-cell compatibility depends on antigens while plasma compatibility depends on antibodies.

Does my blood type affect how much I get paid for plasma?

No. Standard plasma compensation is based on your donation visit, current bonuses, and often your body weight — not your blood type. Two donors of the same weight at the same center earn the same regardless of ABO or Rh type.

Do I need to know my blood type to donate plasma?

No. Plasma centers don't require you to know your blood type for standard donation. You can still donate and get paid as long as you meet the eligibility and screening requirements.

Why is AB plasma so valuable?

AB plasma is universal — it can go to a recipient of any blood type — and only about 4% of people have AB blood, so it's relatively rare. That combination makes it especially valuable for hospital transfusion medicine.

Can O-negative people donate plasma?

Yes. People of every blood type, including O-negative, can donate plasma if they meet the eligibility requirements. O-negative is prized for red-cell donation, but for plasma there's no pay difference based on type.
