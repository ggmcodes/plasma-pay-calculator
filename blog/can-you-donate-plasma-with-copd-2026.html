  • Moderate-severe: Often disqualifies
  • On oxygen: Usually cannot donate

Oxygen levels matter for donation:

  • Normal: 95-100% SpO2 is healthy
  • Acceptable: Most centers want 92%+
  • Borderline: 88-92% may be concerning
  • Low: Under 88% usually disqualifies

Supplemental Oxygen

If you use oxygen:

  • Continuous O2: Usually cannot donate
  • Activity only: Still concerning
  • Sleep only: May affect eligibility
  • Safety first: Don't risk your health

Why Severe COPD Disqualifies

  • Oxygen levels: May not maintain safe O2 during donation
  • Exertion: Even sitting can be tiring
  • Recovery: May not recover well from donation
  • Emergency risk: Higher risk of complications

Tips for COPD Patients

  1. Know your stage: Discuss with pulmonologist
  2. Check O2 levels: Use pulse oximeter at home
  3. Be honest: Disclose COPD during screening
  4. Don't exert: Avoid rushing to appointment
  5. Bring inhalers: Have rescue inhaler with you
  6. Skip if sick: Respiratory infections are serious

Frequently Asked Questions

I have mild COPD and use an inhaler sometimes - can I donate?

Possibly. If your oxygen levels are normal (95%+), you don't use supplemental oxygen, and your COPD is well-controlled, you may be eligible. Be honest during screening.

I use oxygen at night only - can I still donate?

This is borderline. Nighttime oxygen use indicates your body struggles to maintain oxygen levels, which could be problematic during donation. Most centers would defer you.

Will plasma donation make my COPD worse?

Plasma donation itself shouldn't worsen COPD, but the stress and fluid shifts could be challenging for someone with significant breathing difficulties. Prioritize your lung health.

Important

COPD is a serious condition. If you have breathing difficulties, prioritize your lung health over plasma income. There are other ways to earn extra money.
