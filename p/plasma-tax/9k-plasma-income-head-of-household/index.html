Frequently Asked Questions

How much tax do I owe on $9,000 plasma income?

Filing as Head of Household with $9,000 in plasma income, you'll owe approximately $1,272. This includes $1,272 in self-employment tax and $0 in federal income tax.

Is plasma donation income taxable?

Yes, plasma donation income is considered taxable self-employment income. You must report it on Schedule C of your tax return and pay both income tax and self-employment tax (15.3% for Social Security and Medicare).

Do I need to make quarterly tax payments on plasma income?

If you expect to owe more than $1,000 in taxes, the IRS recommends making quarterly estimated payments. For $9,000 in plasma income, we recommend quarterly payments of $318 to avoid penalties.

Will I receive a 1099 form for my plasma donations?

Yes, plasma centers issue 1099-MISC forms for earnings over $600. With $9,000 in donations, you'll receive a 1099 that must be reported on your tax return.

What expenses can I deduct from plasma donation income?

You can deduct legitimate business expenses including: mileage to/from the plasma center (67 cents/mile in 2026), parking fees, iron supplements, and other health-related costs recommended by your center.

How do I report plasma income on my taxes?

Report plasma income on Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business) as self-employment income. You'll also need to complete Schedule SE to calculate self-employment tax. The total flows to your Form 1040.

What is the self-employment tax rate for plasma income?

The self-employment tax rate is 15.3% (12.4% for Social Security and 2.9% for Medicare). This is applied to 92.35% of your net self-employment income. You can deduct half of this tax from your adjusted gross income.

How does filing status affect my plasma income taxes?

Your filing status affects your tax brackets and standard deduction. As a Head of Household, your standard deduction is $21,900. This reduces your taxable income and lowers your income tax (though not your self-employment tax).
